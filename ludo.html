LUDO.

An animated short about sibling love and sacrifice — set on a Ludo board, the game that holds every Pakistani family together.

My Role
Animator · Character Designer · Director
Medium
3D Animation
Theme
Sacrifice · Sibling bond · Family
Setting
A Ludo board in a Pakistani street
"In every Pakistani household, there's a Ludo board that's been played on for decades. The game knows every fight and every reconciliation."

LUDO is a 3D animated short about selfless love — set inside one of the most familiar objects in Pakistani domestic life. The story follows a sister and her younger brother, both depicted as Ludo game pieces journeying across the board. When the brother is threatened by a rolling dice, the sister sacrifices her own freedom — her chance to finish the game — to save him.

The film doesn't explain the metaphor. The Ludo board as a stand-in for family life, the dice as fate, the chains binding both siblings to a yellow house — it all works through visual language alone. No dialogue. Just character performance, sound design, and the mechanics of a game everyone already knows.

Character Design

Game pieces that carry grief.

The central design challenge was making characters that are unmistakably Ludo pieces — toy-like, abstracted, their bodies literally game tokens — while still being able to convey genuine, painful emotion. The solution was hyper-expressive faces on simplified bodies: large eyes capable of full expression sheets, minimal limbs that could still communicate determination, fear, and sacrifice.

Both characters went through full design pipelines — reference research, initial sketches exploring how much "human" to add to an object, proportion studies, and final character sheets including multi-angle views and expression ranges.

Environment

A Ludo board in a Pakistani street.

The environment translates the physical Ludo board into a fully realised 3D world. Surrounding the playing field is a stylised Pakistani street — Urdu graffiti on timber hoardings, hand-painted signs, the specific clutter of a South Asian urban lane. The safe houses at each corner of the board are rendered as yellow structures with corrugated roofs. The paths between them are the same vivid yellow of a real Ludo board.

Story Structure

Six beats. One sacrifice.

1
Established — both trapped
Sister and brother are chained inside the yellow safe house. Both sad. The camera holds on the sister's eyes — wide, alert — as she spots a dice on the ground nearby.
2
The dice rolls — and frees her
The dice lands on six. The sister's chains break. She is free to move. The brother remains chained behind her, watching through the doorway.
3
Temptation — the crown building
She walks out onto the path and sees the crown building in the distance — the finish, the goal, freedom. She begins moving toward it. This is the moment of almost-choice.
4
She looks back
Something makes her turn. A new dice is falling — heading directly for the safe house where her brother sits, still chained. A six would send him off the board entirely.
5
The sacrifice
Determined face. She runs back. Launches herself at the falling dice, deflecting it with her head — friction sparks, blood on her forehead. She flies off the edge of the board.
6
The brother is free
The deflected dice lands on six. His chains break. He steps outside — and sees a cracked Ludo piece lying on the floor beside a table leg. He understands. Fade out.

Reflection

What I learned.

Emotion through abstraction
Making a Ludo piece cry is genuinely harder than making a human character cry. Abstracted characters force precision — every element of the face has to work much harder because there's so little else to carry the emotion.
Cultural specificity grounds the universal
Setting the story in a Pakistani street, using a game specifically tied to South Asian family culture — it transforms a universal story about sibling love into something rooted and particular. The specificity is what makes it feel real.
No dialogue forces better storytelling
Without words, every narrative beat has to be communicated through composition, performance, and sound. That constraint made me a more precise visual storyteller — I couldn't rely on a character explaining what they felt.
Where I'd take it next
A companion short from the brother's perspective — the same sacrifice, witnessed differently. What does freedom feel like when you know what it cost? That's the film I want to make next.
