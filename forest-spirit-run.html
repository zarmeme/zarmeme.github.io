Forest
Spirit Run.

A cozy obstacle course where the level itself is the tutorial — no text, no instructions, just movement and discovery.

Role
Game Designer · Developer · Art Director

Duration
2 weeks

The Question
"How can level design teach mechanics without ever stopping to explain them?"

Most games interrupt flow with pop-up tutorials and button prompts. Forest Spirit Run explores a different approach: what if the environment itself was the teacher? Every gap, platform, and hazard is a sentence in a language players learn to read by moving through it.

Narrative & Tone

Calm urgency.
Move at your pace.

You play as a Kodama — a forest spirit — making its way toward a torii gate. The objective is clear: avoid obstacles, keep moving. But the narrative deliberately resists pressure. There's no timer, no score, no failure state beyond a gentle restart.

That cozy tone is a design decision, not a stylistic flourish. Low stakes give players the psychological safety to experiment — to jump into a gap on purpose, to fall into the water just to see what happens. Curiosity only emerges when failure doesn't feel costly.

Core Mechanics

Four things to learn.
Zero instructions.

↑
Jump
The foundation. Flat terrain and forgiving gaps let players feel the arc and timing with no pressure.
↑↑
Double Jump
A gap that's just too wide for one jump. No hint — just an impossible-looking distance that invites a second press.
〜
Glide
Wider gaps that punish both jump options. Fireflies trace the path. The mechanic reveals itself through curiosity.
💧
Hazards
Water below key challenges makes failure clear and instant. A quick restart keeps the loop tight and low-friction.
Design Approach

The level as curriculum.

Each section of the level is a lesson, sequenced so that earlier mechanics scaffold the next. The progression isn't arbitrary — it mirrors how physical skills are actually learned: simple movement, then variation, then combination under pressure.

1
Jump — feel the arc

Opening terrain is flat and forgiving. Gaps are small enough to clear casually, giving players time to feel the jump arc and timing without consequence. There's nothing to fail here — just movement to internalise.

Double jump — discover through failure

A gap appears that looks almost crossable with a single jump — but isn't. Players who try will fall, restart, and try again. The extra input is never announced; it's waiting to be discovered. When they find it, it feels earned.

Design Principle
Each mechanic is introduced through a problem the player can't solve with what they already know. The gap teaches the jump. The impossible gap teaches the double jump. The impossible-impossible gap teaches the glide. Sequence is curriculum.
Playtesting & Iteration

What the players actually did.

Testing revealed a recurring pattern: players were triggering the double jump too early — pressing it at the peak of the first jump rather than on the way down, causing them to barely clear gaps or miss entirely. The mechanic wasn't broken; the feedback was missing.

The fix wasn't to add a tutorial — it was to make the mid-air state more readable. Jump timing and visual feedback were adjusted to better communicate momentum and position in the arc. After iteration, clearance rates improved significantly without a single word of instruction added.

Reflection

What I learned.

What worked
The level-as-curriculum structure landed. Players discovered all four mechanics through play, with no text UI. The cozy tone genuinely lowered stakes and encouraged experimentation.
What I'd change
Given more time: richer audio-visual cues at each transition, alternative level paths for different player paces, and deeper environmental storytelling to reinforce the Kodama narrative.
The bigger lesson
Feedback is design. The double jump iteration proved that the problem wasn't the mechanic — it was the information gap between what the game was doing and what the player could see.
What this connects to
The same principles that make good onboarding in games — clear feedback, low stakes, sequenced challenge — are the same principles that make good UX. Different medium, same human.
